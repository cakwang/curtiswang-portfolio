tl;dr

An Unreal Engine digital twin of a Kaiser Permanente facility, exploring whether high-fidelity 3D spatial simulation can replace flat compliance training. New hires virtually tour a photorealistic replica of their actual workplace — completing procedural wayfinding tasks instead of reading slides. A comparative pilot was proposed, benchmarking retention from this spatial medium against legacy modalities using Waste Disposal Compliance Training as the test case.

Problem & Learner Context

Most instructional design initiatives begin by diagnosing an existing behavioral deficit. The KP Metaverse project represents a strategic exception: it is a technology-driven exploration designed to uncover the latent instructional possibilities of state-of-the-art tools typically confined to the entertainment industry, specifically Epic Games’ Unreal Engine.

The genesis of this project was partly motivated by an era when the "metaverse" had gained explosive mainstream popularity, rapidly becoming the technological "flavor of the month" across digital media and popular culture. Rather than dismissing the trend as mere hype, this project sought to evaluate its corporate utility: what doors would open if we bypassed standard, flat training staples and recreated entire physical medical offices and facilities from the ground up as open-ended, real-time 3D environments?

Traditional enterprise learning assets—such as static PDFs, slide-based compliance courses, and flat web modules—are perfectly adequate for dispensing explicit content knowledge. However, they completely fail to account for physical 3D spaces and real-time, time-based procedural workflows. Leaving this gap unaddressed means relying on high-friction, real-world facility orientations or abstracting spatial workflows into text-heavy modules that suffer from low long-term retention. Initially designed as a flexible proof-of-concept, the primary target use cases focus on enterprise onboarding (allowing new hires to virtually tour facilities ahead of time) and member-facing wayfinding (helping patients visualize and navigate complex clinics prior to high-stress appointments).

My Design Decisions & Rationale

The decision to utilize Unreal Engine over standard authoring tools was driven entirely by the specific affordances of the technology—namely, the ability to project a user's entire presence into a high-fidelity virtual world to bolster 3D spatial awareness. However, deploying video game mechanics within a diverse corporate workforce introduces a major strategic challenge: the massive variance in digital literacy. Controlling a 3D avatar requires a specific "gaming grammar" (such as using WASD keyboard keys in conjunction with mouse camera manipulation) that is instinctual to active gamers but a jarring friction point for non-gamers.

To bridge this intuition gap without diluting the power of the medium, the design implements an intentional player onboarding framework. Rather than overwhelming users with abstract text descriptions or hidden controller inputs, the system utilizes low-stakes, highly contextualized "side quests" that build fundamental 3D literacy through relatable corporate activities:

  • Learning how to walk around a localized physical corner.
  • Navigating a character from the bottom of a stairwell into a building lobby.
  • Locating a specific assigned cubicle within an office floor plan floor layout.

Project Walkthrough & Highlights

To showcase the spatial accuracy and visual fidelity of the simulation, the final user interface incorporates an interactive slider widget. Hiring managers can drag the slider left and right to see a direct, identical-angle comparison between a real-life photograph of a Kaiser Permanente facility and its digital twin rendered inside Unreal Engine.

Furthermore, to prevent the virtual world from feeling distilled or contrived, the simulation maps minute, granular operational realities into the environment. The walkthrough highlights recurring, culturally relatable Non-Player Character (NPC) behaviors and environmental obstacles, such as an NPC queue waiting to purchase coffee at the lobby Starbucks counter, and a mandatory badge-swipe interaction required to open the main entrance security turnstiles. The closer the simulation adheres to real-life friction and processes, the more grounded and authentic the learning experience becomes.

Results & Evidence of Value

Because this project is in an early, exploratory proof-of-concept stage, tracking standard quantitative data like broad completion metrics would be premature. Instead, the business case and value proposition are established through a proposed comparative pilot framework targeted at a notoriously dry corporate requirement: Waste Disposal Compliance Training.

Traditionally, teaching staff to differentiate between and locate disposal sites for general waste, recycling, biohazardous materials, battery disposal, sharps, and confidential documents is managed via verbose, slide-based compliance courses. Retention is historically low because the content is divorced from physical context. This pilot framework explicitly measures the impact of the simulation against legacy modalities:

Metric Legacy Slide-Based Modality KP Metaverse Simulation
Instructional Approach Linear, text-heavy slides showing asset types and lists. Spatial positioning of assets within a 3D digital twin of the employee's actual clinic.
Engagement Theory Passive consumption driven by extrinsic completion requirements. Active persistence driven by spatial gamification and behavioral simulation.
Hypothesized Outcome Low long-term situational recall; higher operational error rates. "Sticky" long-term procedural retention and reduced real-world disposal errors.

By deploying rapid qualitative questionnaires and post-simulation interviews during this targeted compliance pilot, the project aims to generate concrete, short-term data. This localized evidence will serve as the necessary stepping stone to demonstrate the self-evident benefits of Unreal Engine to senior leadership, clearing the path for more ambitious, large-scale immersive learning pipelines.

Reflection & Lessons Learned

As an active, evolving work-in-progress, long-term success depends on continuously evaluating high-level systemic challenges. The project blueprint actively tracks four open-ended considerations to guide future iterations:

  • The Scalability & Asset Pipeline Bottleneck: Recreating physical facilities from scratch provides unmatched visual fidelity but carries significant developer overhead. Future Focus: Establishing a sustainable asset pipeline that can automate environment creation by ingesting existing architectural CAD or BIM (Building Information Modeling) data, minimizing manual 3D modeling.
  • The "Gaming Grammar" Friction Point: While custom contextual tutorials mitigate initial confusion, some users may continue to struggle with 3D avatar controls. Future Focus: Determining the threshold where control friction overrides instructional value, and developing alternative, ultra-simplified inputs (like a point-and-click navigation alternative) to maximize accessibility.
  • The Fidelity vs. Accessibility Trade-off: Unreal Engine’s cutting-edge rendering requires robust local graphic processors (GPUs) rarely found on standard corporate laptops. Future Focus: Navigating the infrastructure deployment architecture, weighing the costs of cloud-based Pixel Streaming against porting the project to a web-native framework that sacrifices visual polish for universal enterprise access.
  • Isolating Spatial Affordances from Novelty Effects: Initial pilot testing often yields high engagement scores simply because the medium is novel to corporate learners. Future Focus: Designing longitudinal evaluation metrics that track learner performance months after exposure to prove that retention gains stem directly from the 3D spatial architecture rather than a temporary "novelty effect."
